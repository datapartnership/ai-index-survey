Gap identified

Government workers are systematically missing from the list

The most frequently volunteered "missing" category -- raised by 12+ respondents unprompted -- is civil servants using AI for drafting, policy analysis, internal workflows, and coding. One respondent flagged "vibe-coding" by non-technical civil servants as a governance blind spot with direct procurement implications.

"Civil servants using AI for software development support (e.g. coding assistants, vibe-coding) -- knowing how prevalent this is in public agencies would change recommendations on digital capacity-building and procurement guardrails significantly."
Finding 4 | Analytical priorities
Concrete insights for lab partnerships
Sector and task decomposition

The single highest-priority ask: sector + task type breakdowns

The top two weighted statistics both point to the same need: aggregate usage figures are not usable. Respondents need data sliced by what people are doing -- health vs. education vs. government queries -- and by task type (summarization, coding, Q&A, drafting). This is the most concrete, actionable ask for labs.

Government AI usage

Government queries are the #1 specific use case to prioritize

Queries related to government services, regulations, or compliance ranked #1 by 13 respondents. Combined with the "missing use case" signals, both point to the same conclusion: statistics specifically about AI interactions touching government -- citizen-facing and civil servant-facing -- are the highest single-sector priority.

Equity lens

The inequality question is the overarching research concern

The most important single questions cited are almost uniformly about whether AI is narrowing or widening divides -- between income levels, urban/rural users, languages, and education levels. Statistics that speak to inequality of access and quality of AI outputs across country income groups have higher policy leverage than aggregate usage counts.

"Is generative AI reducing poverty at scale in developing countries, or is it widening existing inequalities?"
Professional vs. personal use

Professional/automation signal is a decision-relevant differentiator

The "professional vs. personal / automation vs. augmentation" statistic ranks 4th overall. Respondents designing government reform programs or labor market interventions need to know whether AI is substituting labor or complementing workers -- not just that people are using it.

Missing coverage

Open-source and non-US models are a significant blind spot

Multiple respondents flagged that statistics focused only on US frontier labs miss a large portion of real-world adoption -- particularly Chinese models and the global open-source ecosystem. For institutions advising on technology sovereignty and local AI capacity, this gap is structurally important.

"A lot of our thinking at the World Bank depends on diffusion of open source capabilities, but this has been very challenging to measure quantitatively."
Methodological red flags

Selection bias and representativeness are the top blockers to adoption

43 of 61 respondents cited selection bias; 41 cited representativeness. These are the primary reason respondents said they might not use the statistics in government-facing work. Without credible documentation of who is and isn't captured, the statistics will not survive peer review or policy uptake.

Summary | Recommendations for lab partnerships
Data Contribution Recommendations
Priority 1

Sector and task-type classifications of queries

Labs should produce query-level sector tags (health, education, agriculture, government, finance, labor) and task-type tags (drafting, Q&A, coding, summarization, translation). Even coarse taxonomies would be highly valuable -- these are the highest-ranked statistics by a clear margin.

Priority 2

Government-specific query prevalence by country

A dedicated statistic on the share of queries touching government services, regulations, and public administration -- disaggregated by country -- would directly serve the largest share of respondents. This is the single most-requested specific use case.

Priority 3

Professional vs. personal use signal

Labs should explore probabilistic categorization of queries by professional vs. personal context and automation vs. augmentation intent. Even a rough label would enable labor market and productivity analysis that respondents cannot currently do.

Priority 4

Proxy-based gender and urban/rural breakdowns with uncertainty ranges

90% of respondents will use proxy demographics if confidence intervals are provided. Labs should publish uncertainty estimates alongside proxy estimates -- not withhold data because it is imperfect.

Prerequisite

Methodology transparency package

Before any statistics are used in government-facing work, respondents need: (1) which platforms are included and their market share, (2) confidence intervals on all estimates, (3) validation against external reference data, (4) a public data quality and limitations statement. Without this, uptake will be limited regardless of the statistics produced.

Scope signal

Expand beyond US frontier labs

Multiple respondents explicitly flagged that statistics limited to US commercial labs will be insufficient for global development work. Labs should either track or facilitate tracking of open-source model usage and non-US model adoption -- or acknowledge the gap explicitly in every publication.
